## Claude

Great narrative question — it's a classic noir hole that needs plugging. Here are a few ideas:

**1. He genuinely didn't remember doing it.**
The album didn't just corrupt his morality — it corrupted his memory. Francois hired Tom in complete sincerity because the music had eaten the night of the murder clean out of his mind. He *believed* he was innocent. The horror of the confession scene is that Tom's interrogation doesn't just expose a killer — it forces Francois to remember something he'd lost. The corruption is so total it protected itself by hiding what it made him do.

**2. He wanted to be caught, but couldn't bring himself to confess.**
The part of Francois that hadn't been fully consumed yet knew what he'd done was wrong. But the corrupted part wouldn't let him walk into a police station. Hiring Tom was the last sane fragment of himself reaching out — engineering his own downfall through a back door his corrupted mind hadn't thought to lock.

**3. He thought Tom would be corrupted too.**
Francois knew about the album's power. He'd seen what it did. His plan was never to have the murder solved — it was to expose Tom to the boombox during the investigation, corrupt *him* as well, and gain an ally. Two minds defiled by the same music, bound together. He underestimated Tom's discipline — the fact that Tom only pressed play once, and stopped.

**4. The album told him to.**
The most unsettling option. There's no rational explanation because rationality left the building the moment he pressed play. When Tom asks *why did you hire me*, Francois just smiles and says *it seemed like the right thing to do.* The corruption doesn't just defile logic — it *replaces* it with something that feels completely coherent from the inside. He hired a detective because the music told him that's what innocent people do, and he believed every word of it.

---

Personally I think **1** and **4** are the strongest. Option 1 gives you real pathos at the confession scene. Option 4 is the most chilling and leans hardest into the idea that U2 doesn't just make you do bad things — it makes you *incapable of understanding that you have*.

The uncorrupted fragment is desperate and cornered — it knows what happened, it knows it can't overpower the corruption directly, so it does the only thing it can: it finds a loophole. The ambiguity of whether it was a calculated trick or a genuine delusion is what makes it stick. ---

Tom said nothing. He let it sit in the room. And then, because he was the kind of detective who couldn't leave a loose thread, he asked the question that had been sitting at the back of his mind since the very first day.

*"Then why,"* he said slowly, *"did you hire me?"*

Francois said nothing. He looked out the window at the dark nothing beyond the glass.

Tom watched him. He thought about the office — the urgency, the arguments, the pressure applied in exactly the right places. He thought about the flash of terror that had crossed Francois's face and vanished before it could be named. He thought about a man who had referred to himself in the third person and hadn't seemed to notice. He thought about forty seconds of music moving through him like cold water and what that same thing, given more time, given *much* more time, might do to the architecture of a mind.

And then, slowly, it assembled itself.

Not one man sitting across from him in that office. Two. One that had done something unforgivable and felt nothing about it. And somewhere beneath that — smaller, dimmer, running out of time — something that still could.

Something that had found the only door left open to it.

*"You couldn't confess,"* Tom said quietly. *"But part of you needed someone to find out."*

Francois turned from the window. He looked at Tom for a long moment. Something moved behind his eyes — deep and complicated, like a light seen from a very long way away.

He didn't confirm it. He didn't deny it.

He smiled instead — thin, cold, the smile of a man with nothing left to protect.

*"And now that you know,"* he said, *"you have to die too."*

Now Tom earns it. He reassembles Francois from the fragments he's been collecting since the office — the terror, the third-person slip, the forty seconds on the boombox — and the conclusion arrives as detection, not confession. Francois's silence where an innocent man would have answered is its own kind of answer. And that one flicker of something behind his eyes is the closest the real Francois ever gets to saying thank you.
