Summer. 1997. The kind of heat that sends you inside.

Tom was there. His sister Becky was there — not for much longer; she was leaving for college. Nobody said that out loud. They didn't need to.

They were bored. They were looking for something to do when one of them spotted a disposable camera with a few frames still left on the roll. There were stuffed animals nearby.

This was 1997. Film cost money. Developing cost more. Every frame on that roll was a small commitment — point, shoot, and whatever came back was what you got. No preview. No delete. You paid for it either way.

They used the remaining frames on stuffed animals.

They picked them up. They started posing. Click. Click. Click. And when the last frame was gone — every cent of it spent, the roll finished, the prints out there somewhere waiting to be developed — they made a promise: they'd come back to this. They'd write a story to go with the pictures. They'd see it through.

The promise went quiet.

Twenty-eight years passed.

Some debts don't expire. They just wait — in the back of a drawer, in the corner of a memory, patient in the way only the things you meant to do can be patient. A disposable camera from 1997 doesn't forget. The stuffed animals never moved on. They were still there, the way victims always are: face down, silent, asking the only question that matters.

*Who's going to tell our story?*

This is that story.

Better late than never.

Some would say.
