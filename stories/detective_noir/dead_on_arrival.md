# **Dead on Arrival**
### *A Detective Noir*

---

It was a Monday like any Monday. Grey sky, grey city, grey coffee going cold on the counter. Francois had been away for the weekend — business, he'd say later, though nobody asked twice. He turned his key in the lock, pushed open the door, and stepped inside.

What he saw stopped him cold.

The room had been disturbed. Something was wrong. Something was deeply, fundamentally wrong in a way that a man feels in his chest before his brain has caught up. Francois's legs went out from under him. He grabbed the nearest bedpost and held on like a man clinging to a life raft in a black ocean.

> 📸 *[Photo — Stripes the zebra, wrapped desperately around the bedpost, barely holding himself together]*

He called the police with trembling fingers.

They arrived within the hour — two uniforms, bored on a Monday, expecting a noise complaint or a missing wallet. What they found was Francois, still attached to the furniture, eyes wild, unable to speak in full sentences.

"In there," he managed. "The next room. She's — I can't — just go look."

They looked.

She was on the floor. Face down. Arms out. Not moving. Not breathing. A stuffed ostrich in a teal collar who went by the name Gerald, and who would never again make anyone's acquaintance.

The officers exchanged a glance.

> 📸 *[Photo — Gerald, face down on the carpet, small and still and very much deceased]*

The lead officer snapped his notebook shut. "We'll file a report," he said, in the tone of a man who was already thinking about lunch.

Francois knew then that the police weren't up to this. This wasn't a case for procedure, for forms in triplicate, for men who solved things with clipboards. This case required a different kind of mind. A darker one. One that had stared into the abyss long enough that the abyss had started leaving him voicemails.

There was only one man.

PI Tom.

---

Tom's office smelled like old carpet and bad decisions. He sat behind his desk in his yellow flannel, the look of a man who had seen too much and charged too little for it. Francois laid out the facts. Tom listened with the expression of a philosopher who had concluded, some years ago, that the world was mostly suffering.

He wasn't keen to take the case. He was never keen to take the case. That was part of the thing with Tom — you had to earn it.

Francois pleaded. Tom stared. Somewhere outside, a pigeon made a sound like a slow leak.

Finally, Tom placed his fist under his chin and thought about it for a very long time.


📸 [Photo — Tom, hand to chin, expression unreadable, a zebra riding his head and somehow making it look natural]


"Fine," he said at last. "But I do this my way."

---

Tom worked the case for three days straight. He turned over every rock, rattled every cage, followed every thread until the threads led somewhere ugly. And they always led somewhere ugly.

The conclusion was impossible. He ran the logic again. And again. Same answer, every time.

He went back to Francois.

The interrogation was quiet at first. Tom laid out what he knew, piece by piece, watching Francois's face for the cracks. They came slowly, then all at once. Francois looked out the window at the dark nothing beyond the glass. The stuffed sheep beside him said nothing, because she was a stuffed sheep.

Then Francois's shoulders dropped.

"Yes," he said quietly. "I did it."

He turned to face Tom.

"I strangled her."

📸 [Photo — Stripes and Margaret at the dark window, the moment of confession hanging in the air like smoke]

The silence stretched. Then Francois smiled — thin, cold, the smile of a man with nothing left to protect.

"And now that you know," he said, "you have to die too."

He reached for the nearest weapon.

The scissors.

Of course it was the scissors.

📸 [Photo — Stripes, wielding the enormous scissors with the calm of a man who has rehearsed this moment]

---

Tom moved fast. Faster than a man with a zebra on his head had any right to. They collided — Francois lunging, Tom blocking, the scissors clattering sideways as they grappled across the room in a tangle of flannel and stripes and raw desperation.

All through the skirmish, Tom kept asking. Not shouting — asking. Quietly. Almost gently. The way you ask a question when you already know the answer is going to break something in you.

"Why? How could you do this? What corruption could have possibly entered your mind?"

Francois screamed. Tom held on. They spun. They crashed. The zebra, impossibly, remained on Tom's head throughout.

📸 [Photo — Tom, mouth open, mid-howl, Stripes still aboard, both of them fully unraveled]

Then it came out. The reason.

Simple. Terrible. Obvious in hindsight.

The boombox.

Gerald had been standing on it. Blocking it. Her small fluffy body draped over the U2 CD in the tray, day after day, her feathered form making it physically impossible to press play.

Francois had asked nicely. Several times.

Gerald had not moved. Gerald never moved. Gerald was committed to the floor and, apparently, to blocking access to Achtung Baby.

And Francois had snapped.

📸 [Photo — Stripes, arms spread, performing his heart out on the boombox, U2 CD finally free, finally playing, finally everything Francois had ever wanted]

---

Some time later, Francois lay on the table.

The room beyond the observation window was small and clinical. Tom stood on the other side of the glass, hands in his pockets, watching. There was nothing left to say, and Tom had never been a man who talked when there was nothing left to say.

Francois turned his head. Found Tom's eyes through the glass.

And smiled.

He lifted one small striped arm and waved — slow, cheerful, with the complete serenity of a man at peace with every single choice he had ever made.

"I don't regret a thing," he mouthed through the glass. "It was all worth it. I'd do anything — anything — to hear a second of those sweet, sweet tunes just one more time."

Tom looked at him for a long moment.

Then he turned and walked away down the grey corridor, hands still in his pockets, the sound of U2 faintly playing somewhere behind him.

📸 [Photo — Stripes, tucked under the white blanket, peaceful as a man who has made his peace with the world]

---

Gerald was buried on a Wednesday. It rained.

The boombox still works.

Let this story serve as a warning.
