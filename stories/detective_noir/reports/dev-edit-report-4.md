## New Findings

### 1. Plot Logic and Causality

#### L1. The boombox is absent from the room inventory (line 72 vs. line 80)

**Location:** Line 72: *"The bed with its missing occupant. Scissors. A coffee cup. Three days of unopened mail."* Line 80: *"It sat on the bed like an altar."*

**Issue:** Tom inventories the bed and four objects around it but does not register the boombox, which is sitting on the bed. Two paragraphs later, he turns to it as though it has been there the whole time, and the narrative describes it as sitting on the bed "like an altar" — a visually dominant object.

**Effect on the reader:** The room inventory reads as comprehensive. A boombox on a bed, described moments later as altar-like, would be difficult to overlook while cataloguing the bed's contents. On a careful read, the boombox feels like it materialises when the story needs it rather than being part of the room Tom walked into. This slightly undermines the investigative logic the story has worked hard to establish — the causal chain from Vivian's list to the boombox is sound, but the boombox's physical presence should precede its significance.

**Options:** (a) Add the boombox as one neutral item in the room inventory at line 72 — unnamed or unremarked, just another object on the bed alongside the scissors and coffee cup. The list then gives it meaning, and "He turned to the boombox" becomes a moment of recognition rather than apparent discovery. (b) Have Tom register the boombox when he notes the bed but pass over it without interest — then the list recontextualises what he already saw. Either option requires only a word or short phrase.

#### L2. Francois knows Tom pressed play for exactly forty seconds (line 188)

**Location:** *"You pressed play," he said. "Forty seconds."*

**Issue:** Francois, strapped to a table in the observation room, states with specificity that Tom pressed play and that the exposure lasted forty seconds. The text does not show Tom revealing this information. The confrontation scene says Tom "laid out what he knew, piece by piece" (line 100), which could plausibly include his own exposure — but the specific duration "forty seconds" is a detail Tom would have little reason to share and Francois would have no independent way to know.

**Effect on the reader:** The line is one of the story's most chilling moments. But its power depends on the reader accepting that Francois knows this. A reader tracking the information chain will ask how. If the answer is "Tom must have told him off-page," the moment still works but carries a faint sense of convenience. If the answer is "the corruption somehow knows," the story has introduced a new rule in its final scene without preparation.

**Options:** (a) Accept the gap. The most natural reading is that Tom disclosed his exposure during the confrontation or during the off-page interval ("some time later"), and most readers will not pause to verify. The line's emotional impact likely outweighs the logic cost. (b) Add a single phrase during the confrontation or its aftermath establishing that Tom mentioned pressing play — this closes the gap without drawing attention to it. (c) Remove the specific "forty seconds" from Francois's speech and leave only "You pressed play" — which Francois could more plausibly infer from Tom's demonstrated knowledge of the corruption's effects. The specificity would then belong only to Tom (and the reader), which strengthens the corridor scene's exclusive ownership of that number.

### 2. Foreshadowing and Setup/Payoff

#### F1. The "Tom looked at him" progression is a structural strength (lines 118, 166, 190) — no issue

Three instances of "Tom looked at him" appear across the confession, the fight aftermath, and the observation room. Each modulates differently: "for a moment" (118), "for a long time" (166), "until he was done looking" (190). The progression compresses as Tom's capacity for processing diminishes. This is a quietly effective structural rhyme that rewards attentive reading.

#### F2. The pocket thread continues to resonate (lines 76, 176, 206, 210) — no issue

Noted in Pass 2. Vivian's list is pocketed at line 76. Tom stands with hands in his pockets at lines 176 and 206. The metaphor for recognising an alien thought at line 210 is "a foreign coin in your pocket." Whether intentional or not, this thread connects the evidence Vivian left behind to Tom's moment of self-recognition. It is the story's subtlest piece of subtext and should be preserved.

### 3. Character Consistency and Arc

#### C1. The flash-forward at line 66 slightly tensions against the corridor's dread *(deliberate-risk note, not a flag)*

**Location:** *"He would spend a long time afterwards wondering what exactly had moved him. He never found a satisfying answer."*

**Issue:** This flash-forward implies Tom has a functional "afterwards" — a future long enough to spend wondering. The corridor scene ends with Tom questioning whether he has been corrupted, possibly before the case even began. These two moments are not contradictory — a subtly corrupted man could function and wonder for years — but the flash-forward provides a small reassurance that the corridor then works to erode. The reader enters the corridor believing Tom has a future; the corridor calls that belief into question.

**Note:** This is likely a feature, not a bug. The flash-forward's reassurance makes the corridor's erosion of that reassurance more effective. Flagging it only so the author is conscious of the interaction before final lock.

### 4. Continuity and Timeline

No new continuity issues found. The timeline (Monday discovery, three-day investigation, confrontation within the same week, "some time later" observation room, Wednesday burial) is internally consistent. "His legs went out from under him — the second time that week" (line 156) tracks correctly against the Monday opening. Physical object continuity is clean.

### 5. Theme and Thematic Consistency

#### TH1. The title earns its weight across four appearances — no issue, strength

"Forty seconds" appears at lines 84, 188, 198, and 216. Each instance escalates in meaning: factual duration, accusation, self-recrimination, existential question. The final use ("What if forty seconds wasn't where it started?") redefines the title retroactively — the story was never about forty seconds at all, but about the impossibility of knowing when it began.

**Priority 1 — Plant the boombox in the room inventory (L1).** One word or short phrase at line 72. The boombox sitting on the bed should be visible to Tom (and the reader) before Vivian's list gives it significance.

**Priority 2 — Decide on the "forty seconds" information gap (L2).** Francois's specificity about Tom's exposure duration has no visible source in the text. Three options: accept the gap, bridge it with a phrase during the confrontation, or remove "forty seconds" from Francois's speech so the number belongs only to Tom and the corridor.

**Everything else is working.** The flash-forward/corridor interaction (C1) is flagged for awareness, not action.

**Do not change:** The hiring scene, the corridor walk, the press-play moment, the dual ambiguity, the lyric-bleed positions, the flash of terror, the third-person slip, the legs callback, the observation room scene, the discovery scene re-read signal, the epilogue, the zebra on Tom's head, the confession restraint, the pocket thread, the grey motif.
