## Carry-Forward Items (Still Open)

### CF1. The epilogue redirects from the story's true climax (lines 224–230)

*Flagged: Pass 1 (S4/T3), Pass 2 (S3/T2). Still unresolved.*

**The problem:** The story's emotional and thematic peak is Tom standing in the corridor asking *"What if forty seconds wasn't where it started?"* The epilogue pivots to Francois's funeral, restates the boombox/album status, and closes with a genre-conventional warning. This redirects the reader from the Tom question (am I corrupted?) back to the Francois question (was he villain or hero?) and from existential dread to procedural closure.

**Additional observation for this pass:** Lines 226–228 ("The album has since been classified. / The boombox stays locked up.") restate information already delivered at line 178 ("the boombox was in an evidence locker, sealed, tagged, going nowhere. The album had been classified."). The repetition weakens both instances: the line 178 version is embedded in Tom's emotional processing and carries weight; the epilogue version reduces the same information to bureaucratic summary.

**Options:** (a) Revise the epilogue to touch the Tom question, even obliquely. (b) Cut the epilogue entirely — "Behind him, the observation window went dark" followed by the final image is already a powerful close. (c) Trim the epilogue to remove the redundant lines (226–228) and keep only the funeral line and the warning, which at least avoid restating known information. (d) Reorder so the final line gestures toward Tom rather than Francois.

### CF2. "Everyone he talked to" implies a social world the story has not established (line 72)

## New Findings

### L1. Tom's deduction about Vivian witnessing the initial listen (lines 92–94)

**Location:** *"She'd been there when he first listened. She'd seen what it did to him."*

**The problem:** Tom's reconstruction states as fact that Vivian was present when Francois first listened to the album. The evidence gathered does not support this specific claim. Vivian's list records things Francois *said* over time — it documents behavioral change observed across weeks, not a single witnessed event. "Everyone he talked to said Vivian had been worried about Francois for weeks" also describes a gradual process, not an initial moment.

**Effect:** A detective's reconstruction carries authority. If the reader notices the gap between evidence and conclusion, it weakens trust in Tom's reasoning at the exact moment the story needs his logic to feel airtight — three paragraphs before he confronts Francois.

**Options:** (a) Soften the claim to inference: *"She'd seen what it was doing to him"* (ongoing observation rather than single witnessed event). (b) Add a detail to Vivian's list that would support the stronger claim — one item describing a specific moment of first exposure. (c) Leave as Tom's subjective reconstruction — defensible, but "Because she did know" works against that reading.

### L2. "More people came than had been told to" (line 224)

**Location:** Epilogue, first line.

**The problem:** "More people came than had been told to" implies a controlled, invitation-only funeral. This detail has no setup. It also implies Vivian had a wider social circle that the story has not established.

**Effect:** In a case-file coda, every detail carries the weight of factual report. An unexplained detail in this register reads as either meaningful or careless.

**Options:** (a) Cut the phrase — *"It rained"* is sufficient atmosphere. (b) Replace with something that does not imply a controlled guest list. (c) Keep it as a genre flourish and accept the mild unexplained quality.

### C1. The teal collar's displacement (line 18 vs. line 72)

**Location:** Line 18: *"A stuffed ostrich in a teal collar."* Line 72: *"A teal collar on the floor by the desk."*

**The problem:** In line 18, the teal collar is on Vivian's body when the police find her. In line 72, when Tom investigates on the second day, the collar is on the floor by the desk — separated from Vivian's body and in a different location.

**Effect:** On re-read, a reader tracking physical details will ask: how did the collar get from Vivian's body to the floor by the desk? Possible explanations exist (torn off during strangulation, removed by police) but the text signals none of them. The displacement is potentially meaningful but currently illegible as evidence.

**Options:** (a) Make the displacement intentional and visible: have Tom notice the collar's position relative to where the body was found — a forensic beat that could feed into his deduction. (b) Make the collar consistent across both scenes. (c) Leave it; the attentive re-reader will notice, and if it suggests the violence of the killing, it can work without explanation — but the author should be conscious of the inconsistency.

### C2. Spatial ambiguity in the interrogation scene (lines 98–142) *(lower priority)*

**Location:** "He went back to Francois" (line 98). "The scissors." (lines 138–140).

**The problem:** The interrogation scene does not establish where it takes place. The scissors — established in the crime scene room inventory — being within Francois's reach implies the interrogation happens at or near the apartment. But this is never stated.

**Options:** (a) Add a single orienting phrase at line 98 — "back at the apartment," "in the room where Tom had found her list." (b) Leave it — noir often operates in spatially ambiguous interiors; the scissors are planted at line 72, which is sufficient.

### P1. Lines 16–18: brief omniscient intrusion *(deliberate-risk note, not a flag)*

**Location:** Francois explicitly cannot enter the next room (line 14). Lines 16–18 describe what the police see in that room.

**Note:** This is a clean omniscient intrusion, standard in noir. The camera hands naturally to the police on "They looked." No text change recommended. Update the style sheet to note this as intentional so downstream editors do not attempt to fix it.

### K1. "He thought about" device across four scenes *(deliberate-risk note, not a flag)*

**Location:** Line 74 (investigation), lines 128–130 (deduction), line 176 (observation room), line 200 (corridor).

**Note:** The corridor's version modulates the device across three sentences, which preserves its power. The deduction scene's version is close enough in form and register that the two sequences risk blurring. The author should be conscious of the density. If the deduction scene's version could use slightly different syntactic framing, it would help the corridor land as the device's emotional peak.
